Summary

Methods Used: User journey mapping and clickable prototype

During my time as a trailing spouse in the States, I was fortunate to receive an opportunity to evaluate the process and flow of the soon.works website and offer my insights and recommendations as a design consultant.

About Soon.Works

Soon.Works is a website scheduling tool designed to help businesses efficiently manage their team's schedules. It allows users to create and organize shifts, manage employee leave requests, and streamline daily operations. The platform aims to optimize workforce management by providing features such as shift scheduling, leave management, and task assignment, all accessible through an intuitive and user-friendly interface.

Given that Soon.Works is primarily geared towards scheduling tasks, the team suggests using it on desktop devices instead of mobile. Furthermore, as of now, there is no mobile application offered for Soon.Works. Hence, my redesign is exclusively focused on the web version.

User journey mapping

To gain a deeper understanding of the workflow, I crafted user journey maps for several key processes to identify any potential areas for enhancement.

  1. Signining up to Soon.Works
  2. Setting up of board and team
  3. Getting started

1 - Signining up to Soon.Works

(Video sample on the signing up process)

Opportunities

  • The current sign-up process lacks excitement and allure. It's essential to inject energy and anticipation into the signup page, particularly to captivate potential paying customers, all while ensuring efficient use of white space
  • Instead of employing an outlined border design for the primary button, it would be more effective to enhance its prominence by filling the button with colour
  • Creating a separate page solely for password entry appears unnecessary and adds an extra step. It would be more efficient to integrate it into the same page as the name and email fields to minimize the number of steps required
Wireframes
Redesigned signup

I switched the primary color to purple since it meets the color contrast requirements. Additionally, I integrated the password section into the same screen and utilized the whitespace on the sides to showcase meaningful content, such as customer testimonials.

2 - Setting up of board and team

(Video sample on the setting up process)

Frustrations

  • The setup process requests a team name, yet this information isn't prominently visible or utilized within the application, rendering it redundant to capture
  • The system also requests for a team photo and profile photo, but users using their company's laptop may not have access to such photos. Additionally, many individuals may not upload any image, let alone two
  • The calendar syncing process feels sudden because, as a trial user who has only been using the system for a few days, I'm not yet familiar with its interface. Therefore, I'm unlikely to sync my calendar at this stage since I'm not fully committed
  • Having the setup overlaid onto the main dashboard created a cluttered appearance by drawing attention to the setup and showing elements behind it, resulting in a busy-looking page
Wireframes
Redesigned signup

I eliminated the overlay and introduced a dedicated setup page exclusively for the setup process. Additionally, I replaced the profile picture with the user's initials, indicating that if they don't upload an image, this will be their default representation. This approach alleviates any pressure on users to upload a photo.

The exclusion of the calendar syncing feature from the initial setup process reduces the cognitive load on users. They can access this feature in the dashboard at their convenience to sync their calendars.

3 - Getting started

Viewing of tutorials

(Video sample on the getting started process)

Frustrations

  • My initial impression was that it's overwhelming with information. For an application touted as 'intuitive', having so many guides gives the impression that the system isn't easy to use after all
  • The tutorials being listed per day instead of in a listing format doesn't seem logical. Additionally, they're not clearly categorized, and having to click on 'next week' to view more tutorials creates a mismatch in expectations

Scheduling of tasks

(Video sample on scheduling of tasks process)

Frustrations

  • There were numerous pathways for users to navigate, and they weren't clearly labeled. Relying solely on icons without accompanying text fails to provide sufficient context regarding their significance
  • When creating a task, only a small section is initially displayed. However, users may not realize that clicking on the newly created task will open a large modal for them to fill in additional details.
  • To schedule a task, it opens up in a large popup, potentially leading to double popups when adding people to the task, which is not advisable
  • Lastly, if the screen resolution isn't sufficiently large, some of the seven days will be hidden, necessitating horizontal scrolling. However, the horizontal scrollbar isn't very noticeable, giving the impression of a bug where a day or several days are missing
Wireframes

Final prototype walkthrough

01

Dashboard

Having a dashboard displaying tasks requiring the manager's attention would be beneficial, as it would eliminate the need to sift through all the data to determine priorities. Additionally, implementing a simple guided tour to suggest where to start first would be helpful, given the multitude of tasks in the system, which could potentially overwhelm them.

02

Scheduling of tasks - by day

The new feature enables users to switch between a day view or a week view. Some users may prefer to focus solely on the events of the current day rather than seeing everything at a glance.

Additionally, there will no longer be a large modal for viewing events; instead, they will be displayed on a separate page to avoid double popups.

03

Scheduling of tasks - by week

Drawing inspiration from a MacBook's calendar, where all seven days of the week are visible regardless of screen size, to avoid horizontal scrolling.

Instead of overwhelming users with tutorials from the outset, display them only when a task has been completed. For instance, after a user successfully adds a task, a tutorial will appear to teach them about the drag-and-drop feature.
